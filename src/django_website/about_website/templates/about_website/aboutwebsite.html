About this Website


This website attempts to make predictions based off keyword searches. It is specifically designed for three categories of collectibles: trading cards, comic books and coins. These are all high-end, expensive, professionally graded collectibles, typically around $100.00 and up in value. Below are further descriptions of these categories with specific examples.


Trading Cards


Trading cards in general is a very big subject. There are many different classes and sub-classes of trading cards overall. The most popular class of trading cards are sports cards. They fetch the highest values and professionally graded, high-demand, vintage sports cards are among the most collectible and expensive. The example to the left of the paragraph (image) is a 1979-80 O-PEE-CHEE #18 Wayne Gretzky rookie card, graded by Professional Sports Authenticator (PSA) a 9 MINT condition. Wayne Gretzky Topps and O-PEE-CHEE rookie cards are some of the most expensive, highly popular, collectible hockey cards in the hobby. The main categories of sport cards the draw the the most attention are baseball, football, basketball, hockey, golf and NASCAR racing cards. The most popular grading companies for trading cards are Professional Sports Authenticator (PSA), Beckett grading services (BGS) and Beckett Vintage grading services (BVG), and Sports Card Guarantee (SGC).

The image to the right of this paragraph is an example of a 1980-81 Topps basketball #6 Larry Bird and Magic Johnson dual rookie card (of both Bird and Magic), professionally graded by Beckett grading service (BVG - Beckett Vintage Grading) an 8.5 near mint to mint plus condition. Another highly collectible, vintage, professionally graded card as an investment. Rookie cards almost always tend to the the most valuable, highly collected cards of a certain player. The most valuable vintage cards are almost always inductees into the correspoding sports' Hall of Fame. Rare short prints and very scarce cards take up most of the highly valuable cards, other than rookie cards. Professionally graded sports cards have had an upward trend in value in the past 20 years, while the mid-grade and low-grade counterparts have decreased in value. That even includes vintate, highly popular cards of hall of famers like a 1965 Topps Carl Yastrzemski or even a 1958 Topps Al Kaline.

There are many more highly collectible trading cards than just sport cards. To the left of this paragraph is an image of a 1985 Topps Garbage Pail Kids #8b Blasted Billy. Topps did not make a lot of copies of the first series, from 1985, of Garbage Pail Kids, hence high grades from companies yield hundreds, or even thousands of dollars. Other non-sports trading cards that are popular are Magic - The Gathering, Pokemon, and most any licensed trading cards made before 1970. Goudey made many non-sports sets, issued in packs with bubble gum in a wax wrapper. Topps made many issues of trading card sets during the 50's and 60's, including sets of The Beattles music band, tv shows like Lost in Space, and much more. Even the non-sports tobacco cards from the 19th century command high prices, professionally graded 8 near mint to mint or higher.

Comic Books


Comic books is another huge area of highly collectible, professionally graded items that are bought and sold for investment purposes. To the left is an image of the very popular Walking Dead comic books, issue #1, first edition, graded CGC 9.8 near mint to mint condition. This comic books soared in value when AMC decided to make the very popular television series The Walking Dead. Most highly sought after comic books are vintage comic books, typically first appearances or something called an 'origin'. First appearances in comic books are what rookie cards are to sports trading cards. CGC, Certified Guaranty Company, is probably the most popular and collected grading company in the comic book industry. Comic books are divided up into 'ages. Here are the ages of comic books in chronological order: Golden Age (1938-1956), Silver Age (1956-1970), Bronze Age (1970-1985), Modern Age (1985-PRESENT DAY). The rarest and most valuable come from the Golden Age and Silver Age. These years contain the first appearances of today's most popular comic book stars. Comic books in general took a big down turn from the mid 1990's to the early 2000's, along with all the other mass produced collectibles of the times including trading cards, coins, toys, just about anything that was really valuable if it was made before World War II. Comic books, took a big upturn starting around 2003 due to all the movies that were being made about comic book heroes and 'leagues'. CGC also caters to specifc 'pedigrees' of comic books, such as the 'Mile High Collection'. Many special pedigrees of comic books fetch higher prices given the pedigree reputation.

To the left of this paragraph is a CGC professionally graded Golden Age Batman #1, the first appearance of 'The Joker'. The resurgence of movies and television shows based off comic books gave a huge boost to the older, most valuable first appearance and origins of the most famous comic book heroes. Many of these movies and shows featured comic book heroes from whats called the 'Marvel Universe'. In comic books, the two most popular makers of comic books are Marvel and DC. Those two makers feature their own 'Universe' of comic book heroes and villains. The Marvel Universe includes heroes and villains such as Spider-Man, Thor, Dr. Strange, Deadpool, Iron Man, Silver Surfer, Captain America, Woliverine, Dr. Doom, Magneto, Red Skull, Loki, Ultran and countless more. The Marvel Universe also includes very famous groups such as The X-Men, The Fantastic Four, The Avengers and Justice League of America. The 'DC' Universe has Superman, Batman and Robin, Flash, Green Lantern, Wonder Woman, The Joker, Lex Luthor, Deathstroke, Two-Face, The Riddler and Catwoman just too name a few. Both Universes are huge with countless memorable heroes, villains and groups of either.

Stan Lee is the most popular writer, and made comic books for Marvel. Stan Lee has a cameo in almost all movies, about Marvel universe heroes/villains. The image to the left is a Silver Age 'The Indredible Hulk' #1, first appearance of the Hulk, professionally graded by CGC a 5.5 Fine minus (or Excellent minus condition). The Incredible Hulk, for instance, has had three major actors play him in the past 16 years or so. The actors playing the Hulk in a movie include: Eric Bana, Edward Norton and Mark Ruffalo. Comic books started to really pick up value, in the 1970's, when some major motion pictures were made of comic book heroes. Christopher Reeve played Superman back in the late 1970's and early 1980's. That time period saw a huge rise in the value of Action Comics #1 (first appearance of Superman) and early Golden Age issues, as well as important issues in the DC Universe. Michael Keaton played Batman in the 1989 movie, seeing a huge rise in the 'Detective Comics' #27 (first appearance of Batman). Comic books will continue to see rising auction prices for high grade first appearance and origin issues from the Golden Age and Silver Age. These comic books, professionally graded, deserve price predictions using Data Science prediction models.

Coins/Numismatics


PCGS (Professional Coin Grading Services) and NGC (Numismatic Guaranty Corp.) are the most popular professional grading services for U.S. coins. The a big percentage of the most popular coins are going to be U.S. and U.S.A. coins. To the left is an image of a beautiful 1950 Benjamin Franklin Half dollar, professionally graded by NGC an Proof-67 grade with 'cameo' quality (rare, of course). The 'cameo' makes it extra special, and make it stick out from the other proof-65 and proof-64 copies with no 'cameo' in the proof surface. Collecting U.S. coins in a hobby that goes back at least a century. Coin collecting really gained momentum in the 1960's when the United States start making silver certificates and coins with silver content (with the exception of silver clad JFK half dollars circa 1965-1969). Children all over the country would go to the bank, change in paper currency for a bag of coins, and look for errors like the 1955 'Double Die' Lincoln wheat penny or the 1937-D 'three legged' Buffalo nickel. My father was one of these children back in the 1950's and 1960's. It was around this time that there was a major rise in the value of rare coins and paper currency. Collecting foreign coins is also a huge percentage of coin collecting as well. This includes everything from old Roman coins to Spanish Reals.

The image to the left shows one of the most valuable coins in U.S. history. I very rare 1913 Liberty Head V Nickel, graded by PCGS as Proof-66. A very difficult grade to get for a proof minted coin from over 100 years ago. A coin like this would fetch around $4,000,000.00 or so. I (John Heinz) collected coins as a child, not just sports cards and comic books. I would look for hours at coins, under my jeweler loop my dad bought me, and look for errors that would make the coin valuable. Not all coins with a doubled die or an error are worth thousands of dollars. The industry determines which errors are really valuable and no mention of other coins with errors. There are so many highly collectible types of U.S. coins. Morgan dollars are really popular, gold double eagles, Seated Liberty dimes and half dollars, Standing Liberty quarters, Walking Liberty halves, the list goes on. Collecting albums, getting all the mint marks for a year, was very popular many decades ago. The only thing that seemed to stand the test of time, like trading cards and comic books, are the really rare, extra high grade, professionally graded 'Holy Grails' of the hobby.

To the left is a rare U.S. 1800 $10 gold eagle Draped Bust (not mint mark typically means minted in Philadelphia). The coin was professionally graded by PCGS a AU-53 ('AU' means 'About Uncirculated').
